Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks, Age, Income and Poverty

One of the most important correlations founded is between household income and each of the health risks that appear in the dataset. The figure shows that if the household income increases the percentage of people with obesity, smokers and the ones that lack healthcare appears to decrease. That is, the figure shows the existence of a negative correlation between household income and health risks. Also, it can be seen that the states that have the highest income are Washington, Alaska, New Jersey, and Maryland.

On the other hand, the figure shows another relevant correlation between poverty and each of the health risks that appear in the dataset. The results indicate that as the percentage of poverty increases each of the health risks appears to increase as well. That is, a positive correlation is found between poverty and every health risk. Mississippi, Lousiana, and New Mexico are the states with the highest poverty percentage.

Finally, something that worth saying is related to age. This factor does not appear to correlate to any of the health risks contained in the dataset either positive or negative. Further analysis needs to be done to calculate the Pearson coefficient and therefore determine the level of correlation between factors. Meanwhile, the graphs suggest the presence of some clear correlations.
